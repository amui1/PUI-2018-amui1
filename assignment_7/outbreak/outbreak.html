Oubreak

User Testing - Data Analysis

January 2017 - December 2017

Outbreak is part of CMU HCII's Sensing Curiosity in Play Responding (SCIPR) project. This project focuses on fostering scientific curiosity in middle school students through tabletop board games. Outbreak, in particular, aims to encourage question asking and comfortableness with short term failure in students ages 9 and up.

I first worked as a researcher and playtest coordinator. I was primarily responsible for running user tests every weekend from January to March. This included greeting parents, taking care of participants for the day, and running through the study protocol from beginning to end. During the week, I was responsible for analyzing field notes with the rest of the Outbreak team.

Outbreak is a race-the-clock, card matching, hidden information game for 3-5 players ages 9 and up. It is collaborative: either everyone wins, or everyone loses. Players, as a team of research scientists, move throughout the game board, encountering threat rooms. The scientists have access to a sensing robot and ask it questions in order to deduce what is inside.

For data analysis, our team focused on identifying relationships between emotion and specific game events, such as winning a round, getting into a team disagreement, or contributing a thoughtful question. We collected emotion quantitatively through a QEM map and specific observations documented in round-by-round field notes. Quantitative field observations included number of questions asked, number of conflicts and agreements, or number of rounds lost. Qualitative field observations included type of expression or phrases said. Other information was collected through a pre-study questionnaire on curiosity and post-study questionnaire on demographics.

Currently, I am contributing to Outbreak as a co-author for the paper. The paper will be submitted to the Interaction Design & Children Conference in January.
